Security+ CIA Triad

Confidentiality

Confidentiality ensures that data is accessible only to authorized individuals and is protected from unauthorized access. It is a critical component of the CIA triad, focusing on maintaining the privacy and secrecy of sensitive information.

  • Encryption: Protects data by converting it into an unreadable format using algorithms like AES or RSA. Only those with the correct key can decrypt and access the data.
  • Access Controls: Mechanisms like passwords, biometrics, and multi-factor authentication (MFA) ensure only authorized users can access systems or data.
  • Data Classification: Categorizing data based on sensitivity (e.g., public, confidential, top-secret) to apply appropriate security measures.
  • Secure Communication Protocols: Protocols like TLS/SSL (e.g., HTTPS on port 443) encrypt data in transit to prevent interception.
  • Role-Based Access Control (RBAC): Assigns permissions based on user roles, limiting access to what is necessary for job functions.
  • Data Masking: Obscures sensitive data (e.g., replacing credit card numbers with asterisks) to prevent exposure during processing or testing.
  • Physical Security: Measures like locked server rooms or secure facilities prevent unauthorized physical access to systems storing sensitive data.

Confidentiality is vital in industries like healthcare (e.g., HIPAA compliance) and finance, where data breaches can lead to significant privacy violations. Common threats include eavesdropping, social engineering, and insider threats.

Integrity

Integrity ensures that data is accurate, complete, and trustworthy. It prevents unauthorized modifications, whether intentional or accidental, to maintain the reliability of information.

  • Hashing: Uses algorithms like SHA-256 to create a unique fingerprint of data, allowing detection of any unauthorized changes.
  • Digital Signatures: Verifies the authenticity and integrity of a message or document using public/private key pairs, ensuring it hasn't been altered.
  • File Permissions: Restricts who can modify data, preventing unauthorized changes (e.g., read-only access for certain users).
  • Version Control: Tracks changes to data or code, allowing rollback to previous versions if unauthorized modifications occur.
  • Checksums: Validates data integrity during transfers by comparing computed values to detect corruption or tampering.
  • Backup and Recovery: Regular backups ensure data can be restored to a known good state if integrity is compromised.
  • Change Management: Formal processes to control and document changes to systems or data, reducing the risk of unauthorized alterations.

Integrity is crucial for systems like financial transactions or medical records, where altered data can lead to severe consequences. Threats to integrity include malware, unauthorized access, and human error.

Availability

Availability ensures that systems, applications, and data are accessible to authorized users when needed. It focuses on maintaining operational systems and protecting against disruptions.

  • Redundancy: Implements failover systems, such as clustered servers or backup power supplies, to ensure continuous operation during failures.
  • Disaster Recovery (DR): Plans and systems to restore operations after incidents like natural disasters or cyberattacks (e.g., ransomware).
  • Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) Protection: Mitigates attacks that overwhelm systems, ensuring resources remain accessible to legitimate users.
  • High Availability (HA) Systems: Uses load balancers and failover clusters to minimize downtime (e.g., 99.999% uptime).
  • Regular Maintenance: Applies patches and updates to prevent vulnerabilities that could disrupt system availability.
  • Network Security: Firewalls, intrusion detection systems (IDS), and secure configurations (e.g., closing unused ports like 23/Telnet) prevent attacks that disrupt services.
  • Scalability: Ensures systems can handle increased demand, such as during traffic spikes, to maintain performance and access.

Availability is critical for services like e-commerce or emergency systems, where downtime can lead to financial loss or safety risks. Common threats include DDoS attacks, hardware failures, and power outages.
